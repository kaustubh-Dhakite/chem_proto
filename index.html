Step-by-Step

Preparation Procedure

1

🧴 Gather Materials & Equipment

Collect a 1-litre graduated bottle, ethanol (96%), hydrogen peroxide (3%), glycerol (98%), and sterile distilled water. Ensure all glassware is clean and dry before starting.

2

🔬 Pour Ethanol into Mixing Vessel

Measure and pour 75.15 ml of 96% ethanol into the graduated mixing bottle. This forms the primary antimicrobial base of the formulation.

3

⚗️ Add Hydrogen Peroxide

Add 4.17 ml of 3% hydrogen peroxide to the bottle. This deactivates potential contaminating spores in the formulation container.

4

💧 Add Glycerol

Carefully add 1.45 ml of 98% glycerol using a sterile measuring pipette. Glycerol is viscous — rinse the measuring pipette with sterile distilled water once or twice.

5

🌊 Top Up with Distilled Water

Add sterile distilled water to bring the total volume up to 100 ml. Use the graduation marks on the bottle for precision.

6

🔄 Mix Thoroughly

Immediately cap the bottle and shake it gently but thoroughly for 30–45 seconds. This ensures even distribution of all components in the final mixture.

7

⏳ Quarantine for 72 Hours

Set aside the filled bottle(s) for at least 72 hours before use. This quarantine period allows hydrogen peroxide to act on any bacterial spores in the bottles.

8

🏷️ Label & Store

Label the product with composition, date of preparation, batch number, and expiry date. Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and heat sources.

Uses

Applications of Hand Sanitizer

🏥

Hospitals & Clinics

Used by healthcare workers between patient interactions to prevent hospital-acquired infections and cross-contamination.

🏠

Home Use

Convenient for disinfecting hands before meals, after outdoor contact, or when soap and water are unavailable.

✈️

Travel

Compact and portable for airports, trains, and transit hubs where hygiene facilities may be limited.

🏫

Schools & Colleges

Deployed at entry points and classrooms to reduce spread of seasonal illnesses among students and staff.

🍽️

Food Industry

Food handlers use it to maintain hygiene standards and prevent microbial contamination in food preparation areas.

🏢

Offices & Workplaces

Placed at reception desks, meeting rooms, and shared equipment stations to promote employee wellness.

🛒

Retail & Public Spaces

Supermarkets, shopping centres, and public venues use dispensers at entry and exit points for mass hygiene.

🌍

Emergency & Disaster Relief

Critical in disaster zones or refugee camps where clean water is scarce and infection risk is elevated.

Comparison

Advantages & Disadvantages

✅ Advantages ⚠️ Disadvantages
+Quick and convenient — no need for water or towels to rinse.
−Not effective against all pathogens — e.g., norovirus, C. diff spores.
+Kills 99.9% of bacteria and most common viruses within 30 seconds.
−Flammable due to high ethanol content; must be stored away from heat/flame.
+Portable — easy to carry in bags, pockets, and travel kits.
−Can cause skin dryness or irritation with very frequent use.
+Reduces transmission of infectious diseases in public settings.
−Toxic if ingested — especially dangerous for children; must be stored safely.
+Cost-effective when prepared in bulk using the WHO formulation.
−Does not remove dirt, heavy metals, or pesticide residues from hands.
+Minimal equipment needed — can be prepared in resource-limited settings.
−Effectiveness decreases if applied in insufficient quantities or on wet hands.
Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Ethanol (ethyl alcohol) is preferred because it has proven broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity, is less toxic to skin than methanol or isopropanol at equivalent concentrations, and is accepted by WHO global formulation guidelines. It denatures proteins effectively at 70–80% concentration.
Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) serves as a preservative rather than an active antiseptic in this formula. At the concentration used (0.125%), it inactivates any bacterial spores that may be present in the bulk ethanol or on the manufacturing equipment, ensuring a sterile final product.
Glycerol is a humectant that draws moisture to the skin. Because ethanol can be drying with repeated use, glycerol counteracts this by keeping the skin hydrated, preventing cracking, and improving user compliance with regular hand hygiene habits.
Research and WHO guidelines indicate that a minimum final concentration of 60–70% ethanol (or 60–65% isopropanol) is required to effectively kill most bacteria, fungi, and enveloped viruses. The WHO formulation achieves approximately 75% final ethanol concentration.
Yes. Alcohol-based hand sanitizers containing ≥60% ethanol are effective against enveloped viruses including influenza and SARS-CoV-2. The alcohol disrupts the lipid membrane of these viruses, rendering them non-infectious. However, non-enveloped viruses like norovirus are more resistant.
Always work in a well-ventilated area away from open flames or sparks, as ethanol is highly flammable. Wear gloves and lab coat. Avoid inhaling vapours. Keep hydrogen peroxide in its original container away from organic materials. Label all containers clearly and keep out of reach of children.
When stored in a sealed container at room temperature away from light and heat, the WHO hand sanitizer formulation remains effective for up to 2–3 years. Effectiveness decreases if the container is left open, as ethanol evaporates over time, reducing the active concentration.
Chemistry

Chemical Reaction & Mechanism

🦠

Protein Denaturation

Ethanol disrupts hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions within bacterial proteins and enzymes. This causes irreversible unfolding (denaturation), rendering the proteins non-functional and killing the cell.

🔴

Lipid Membrane Disruption

Ethanol dissolves the phospholipid bilayer of microbial cell membranes, increasing permeability. This causes leakage of intracellular contents (ions, amino acids, nucleotides), leading to rapid cell lysis and death.

⚡

DNA Denaturation

At high concentrations, ethanol penetrates the cell and disrupts nucleic acid structure, preventing DNA replication and transcription. This halts cell reproduction entirely, stopping the spread of infection.

🌊

Oxidative Damage (H₂O₂)

Hydrogen peroxide generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) — particularly hydroxyl radicals — that oxidise DNA, proteins, and lipids inside microbial cells, causing wide-spectrum antimicrobial damage.

Key Chemical Reactions
C₂H₅OH (Ethanol) + Microbial Proteins → Denatured Proteins + Cell Death

H₂O₂ → H₂O + [O]   (Nascent oxygen — highly reactive)

[O] + Microbial DNA/Lipids → Oxidised & Non-functional Biomolecules
